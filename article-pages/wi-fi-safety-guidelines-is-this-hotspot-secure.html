Wi-Fi Safety Guidelines: Is This Hotspot Secure?

Back in 2017, an American software company called Symantec conducted a Wi-Fi Risk Report and found that very few Wi-Fi users were concerned about safety when they logged in to a public Wi-Fi network or hotspot. The most important thing for users is just to be connected as quickly as possible. But, using any public network comes with certain risks, such as identity theft or spying. Here are a few ways for you to check that the network you’re on is secure. Ask yourself these questions…

Is this the correct network?

Make sure you access the right network by choosing the correct name. Often, public spaces like restaurants, hotels or malls will have multiple networks available – for guests or employees etc – so it can be tricky to know which hotspot you should connect to. The more similar-looking names there are, the easier it is for hackers to create their own similar network in order to get your information. If you’re not sure, ask someone in charge and get them to write the name down for you. Normally, open Wi-Fi networks will seem like the most convenient choice, but it’s safer to sign in properly with the correct password – on the correct network.

Did I ask to join this network?

Often, if you’ve logged in to a network before – and you have your smartphone or computer Wi-Fi turned on – the device will automatically find and log in to that network. But, if you’ve used it before, it should be secure. The problem comes when your device automatically logs in to a network you haven’t signed into before. This could be an unsafe hotspot. Make sure you’ve set your phone or computer to ask you if you want to join a network. The setting will probably be under Advanced Wi-Fi Settings on your device. Rather than allowing it to ‘automatically connect’, set it to ‘ask to join a network’. That way, you’ll always know which network your device is connected to.

Do I need a password?

Make sure that if the person in charge gives you a password to access the public Wi-Fi network, you have, in fact, entered it. If it sounds too good to be true, and the network isn’t asking for a password, it probably is too good to be true and you’re on an unsafe hotspot. People won’t use passwords unnecessarily, so if you’ve been given one, be sure to use it.

What shouldn’t I do on a public hotspot?

If you’ve accessed a public network, you should probably be a bit more aware than if you were using your personal Wi-Fi at home. Sure, you can browse the internet, check your social media pages, do some work or research topics. But avoid completing any very personal tasks where you have to enter personal data. Things like paying bills, accessing your bank account or banking app, and even paying your taxes require you to enter very private information. It’s best to do this on your own private network.

Conclusion

Yes, we all want to stay connected and keep up with our social and professional lives wherever we are. But, before you risk being hijacked or spied on, ask the smart questions. It’s important to protect yourself and stay safe out there.
