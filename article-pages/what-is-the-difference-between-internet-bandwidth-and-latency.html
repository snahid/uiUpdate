What is the Difference Between Internet Bandwidth and Latency

Are you looking for an internet service and hear terms being thrown around without knowing quite what they mean? For example, bandwidth and latency are two words that you will hear quite often when discussing internet service. However, sometimes these terms can be a bit confusing, especially to someone who is new to the internet scene. When talking about internet services, it is essential that you know the difference between the two and understand how they work together.

What is Bandwidth?

Imagine the water pipes running into your home. The wider the pipe, the more water your home can get at a given time. That is what bandwidth is with respect to the internet (bigger pipes mean more data can be transferred). There are two dimensions to bandwidth; download and upload. Downloading is when you take data from the internet and store it on your local system. Uploading is when you take data from your system and load it to the internet.

What is Latency?

Back to the water pipe example, bandwidth was how much water could come through the pipe at a given time. Latency is how fast that water comes through the pipe. For internet speeds, it is precisely that same scenario: data speeds through your internet pipe. For example, if you have a latency of 100 milliseconds, that means your data is traveling from your device to the internet and back in 1/10th of a second.

How Does Bandwidth and Latency Work Together?

This is where internet speed, or throughput, comes in. When talking about how "fast" an internet connection is, what this really means is how quickly you can download something, like a song or a movie. If you have a 100-megabyte file and you can download 100 megabytes per second, then your "speed" is 100 megabytes per second. Internet providers measure their internet speeds in a slightly different way. It is done in megaBITS rather than BYTES.

How Bits and Bytes Work

When you hear an internet provider talking about offering 100mpbs internet speeds, they are taking about megabits per second. When talking about the size of your specific files, it is discussed in megaBYTES, not megaBITS. There are 8 bits in a byte, so if you have an 8 bit per second internet connection, that translates to 1 byte per second. For instance, if a cable internet company offers an 800mpbs (megaBITS per second) connection, that translates to 100 megaBYTES per second. So, if the movie you want to download is 100 megabytes in size, then an 800mpbs connection will download that movie in 1 second.

Conclusion

Bandwidth and latency are often terms used interchangeably, but they are certainly not the same thing. They can be a bit confusing, but as you can see, they are both critical components for quality internet. Understanding how these aspects work and operate together is something you will want to be familiar with when discussing internet services so you can get the best option available to you.
