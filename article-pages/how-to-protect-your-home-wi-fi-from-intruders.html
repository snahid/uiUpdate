How to Protect Your Home Wi-Fi From Intruders

You probably have a Wi-Fi router in your home that provides you internet access to you and the rest of your family. When you have friends over, maybe you give them your Wi-Fi password so they can enjoy the access as well. However, sometimes people who you did not permit your Wi-Fi access to end up using it anyways, because, well, they are hackers. In order to prevent yourself from falling victim to your Wi-Fi being intruded, take the following precautionary measures so you can have a safe and secure system.

Use complex passphrases

First and foremost, set complex passphrases to access your Wi-Fi connection. The recommended criteria to following from multiple security standards are:

  • 14 characters long
  • Uppercase letters of European languages
  • Lowercase letters of European languages
  • Base 10 digits (0 through 9)
  • Special characters (~!@#$%^&*_-+=`|\(){}[]:;"'<>,.?/)

Passphrases that use this criterion are exponentially harder to crack than something simple like “P@ssW0rd”. In fact, the use of software to “brute force” attack your Wi-Fi router with combinations of letters numbers and special characters could crack a password like that in no time.

Use the Highest Level of Encryption

WPA2 AES encryption is a must to secure your Wi-Fi. Most devices these days are compatible, and if they are not, get rid of them and buy ones that are. It is not worth the security risk that WEP and WPA encryption methods pose. Encryption makes it far more difficult for hackers to figure out what your passphrase is, even if they get the encrypted version of it.

Hide Your Broadcasted Wi-Fi Name (SSID)

One straightforward method to secure your Wi-Fi connection is hiding your broadcasted Wi-Fi name or SSID. When you drive by a house or business, you may see a Wi-Fi network appear on your phone or laptop. That is a broadcasted Wi-Fi network that lets people know you have one. Login to your router or access point and find the option to hide your SSID so unauthorized people will no longer be able to see your network broadcast in plain text.

Limit Devices That Can Connect

Limiting the devices that can connect to your Wi-Fi is another good way of controlling who can access your network. Most home network equipment can whitelist which devices are allowed to connect to your network. All other devices, even with the passphrase and SSID in hand, will not be allowed in.

Survey Your Area

Walk around the inside and outside your house to see how far the Wi-Fi signal reaches. If your Wi-Fi signal reaches your neighbor's home to the north but the southern side of your house has a weak signal, move the Wi-Fi router close to the south side of your house. If people are not in range of your Wi-Fi router, they will not be able to attempt access.

Conclusion

Wi-Fi systems are not like physical networks. They expand beyond the walls of your home, and it can be hard to control who can get access if you are not taking the proper precautions. By implementing the tips above, you should be able to have a foul proof Wi-Fi system that will keep intruders out, so the access is only being utilized by the people it was intended for.
